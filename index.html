Abstract

Learning visual representations with interpretable features, i.e., disentangled representations, remains a challenging problem. Existing methods demonstrate some success but are hard to apply to large-scale vision datasets like ImageNet. In this work, we propose a simple post-processing framework to disentangle content and style in learned representations from pre-trained vision models. We model the pre-trained features probabilistically as linearly entangled combinations of the latent content and style factors and develop a simple disentanglement algorithm based on the probabilistic model. We show that the method provably disentangles content and style features and verify its efficacy empirically. Our post-processed features yield significant domain generalization performance improvements when the distribution shift occurs due to style changes or style-related spurious correlations.

Overview

Pre-trained vision models produce feature representations that mix together content (the semantic, label-relevant information) and style (texture, color, domain-specific appearance, and other factors that vary across environments). When the style of the data shifts — for example, moving from photos to sketches or paintings — models that rely on entangled features tend to generalize poorly.

This paper introduces a lightweight post-processing approach that sits on top of an already pre-trained vision backbone. Rather than training a new disentangled representation from scratch — which is difficult to scale to large datasets such as ImageNet — the method models existing pre-trained features as linearly entangled combinations of latent content and style factors, and recovers each factor with a simple algorithm derived from this probabilistic model. The authors prove that, under their modeling assumptions, the algorithm provably separates content from style, and they show empirically that the resulting content-only features substantially improve domain generalization when distribution shift is driven by style changes or by style-related spurious correlations.

Why it matters

  • Scalable: works as a post-processing step on top of existing pre-trained models, avoiding the cost of training disentangled representations from scratch on large datasets like ImageNet.
  • Theoretically grounded: comes with provable disentanglement guarantees under a simple linear probabilistic model of how content and style combine.
  • Practically useful: the disentangled (content-only) features lead to measurable improvements in domain generalization under style-driven distribution shift and style-related spurious correlations.
